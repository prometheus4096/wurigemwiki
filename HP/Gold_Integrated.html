Our idea

We are making a diagnostic device to test humans for tropical diseases in remote areas. Our labwork is clear, how we implement this diagnostic is not. We have multiple diseases with which we can do the screening; both viral and parasitic antigens. How do we make this into one story? How can we visualise our ideas the best? How should our device look like? Is this even the best approach? We talked to several experts and integrated their expertise into our project.

The story so far

Our device is going to be implemented for individual testing on tropical diseases. We have three scenarios to sketch, to visualise the modularity of our device: An neglected, long-known epidemic (HAT), a recent outbreak (Zika virus) and a disease which might cause an outbreak in the near future (Mayaro).

Prof. Joseph Ndung'u

Programme Head at FIND, expert in HAT

We wanted to get a more complete overview of the situtation in the field in Africa. This had mainly to do with the HAT situation, this was his main interest. He basically told us that the field teams drive hours into the countryside to test possible patients, mainly using dipsticks. The field teams have next to no facilities. Therefore the test should be rapid and cheap and easy to use. Tested people are often sceptical about the results as they do not feel ill and thus think they don’t need treatments. The availability of tests and the ability of field teams to reach people that need the tests is highly dependent on the current political situation in the country. Jan 24

Jeanine Loonen

Medical Entomologist, Doctors Without Borders

Sudden explosions in the number of infected people may be caused by mosquitos that get resistant against pesticides. Control of malaria is challenging because political status of Congo: setting up and maintaining a functional health care system is difficult. DWB negotiate with both parties (governmental militia and rebel groups), they acchieved building a hospital that acts as a base (prepare for field work) and it attracks local population that go to this hospital in stead of to local health centers of the government. The hospital employs both foreign specialist and locals, in which the former educates the latter. The goal is to create an independent health care system, run by experienced locals, without the interference of Doctors Without Borders. Furthermore, DWZ also has to rely on the help of locals for the delivery of (some) medicine and equipment in case their shipments cannot be delivered to the hospital due to dangerous situations caused by the country’s conflicts. Locals are then asked to take some of the DWZ provisions along on their travels across the country. DWZ outreach teams are active in screening for malaria in mosquitos as well as in the population, but also by educating locals about the work they do. The latter is very important to gain the trust of the population and prevent miscommunications. In case outreach teams are testing the population for malaria they will carry rapid malaria diagnostics, which give a read-out in 15 minutes. If positive, the patient will be taken along to the DWZ hospital. Introduction of a new diagnostic in countries such as DRC requires trust by the government, as they need to give permission always. Therefore, it is best to do this via established NGOs such as DWB. On the topic of diagnostics for diseases without a cure or treatment, Jeanine mentioned that these are still very important, as they give information about the diseases present in the area, as well as their role in preventing epidemics. When asked for the view of locals on GMOs Jeanine couldn’t give a sure answer, as she was not fully aware of their opinions.

Aug 11

Marianne van der Sande

hoofd van het centrum Epidemiologie en Surveillance van Infectieziekten, RIVM

Successful use of diagnostics depends on the disease, but it is required to assess how it spreads. Continued use through the progress of the epidemic . However, it is only one of the tools used at the human disposal. Apply diagnostics to identify the disease and come up with appropriate measures (treatment, surveillance). After the big peak of the epidemiology, diagnostics are still applicable (depends on the pathogen) but only risk groups relevant for the disease will be diagnosed. This requires characterization of the disease. Costs are not the most important (due to donors and investments by the West). Should have reproducible specific results, robust in tropical settings (both temperature, light intensity and humidity). Ease of use plays a big role too. Prevalence of a disease only becomes clear by applying a diagnostic. Predicting where a disease might emerge is near useless, since it is a chance scenario. A modular tool sees use when there is an outbreak of an pathogen where no convenient diagnostic already exists. The device should have advantages that make it attractive to use (cost-wise, ease of use, etc.). Collaboration between different divisions to tackle the infectious problem is one of the most important factors to consider. Groups that will have to work together, are social scientists, clinicists, epidemiologists among others. Set up laboratoria in the area of operation and acquire licenses to do so (may be hard due to the fact that third-world countries cannot pay for these themselves). Negotiations with the private companies should solve this in case of a global problem. Predicting outbreaks Hard due to the fact that pathogens have a diverse effect on each segregation of the human population, because of the different genetic makeup of each different person and corresponding resistances.

Aug 18

Robert-Jan Hassing

Mayaro Virus/ Viral infections in developed countries, Rijnstate Velp

In 2008, a Dutch couple infected with Mayaro came back from Surinam. They were treated in Rotterdam Hospital, RJ Hassing was responsible for this among others and wrote a paper on this particular case: “Imported Mayaro Virus infection in the Netherlands (2010)” the couple showed mainly very common symptoms for viral infections, and most likely they were infected with either Dengue or Chickungunya, both very similar viruses. However serological tests proved that this wasn’t the case. Taking into account the areas where similar but obscure viruses are present, Mayaro proved to be the most logical candidate and after obtaining assays against Mayaro, this suspicion was proven to be right. Assays againt obscure diseases are difficult to obtain. Most of them are stored in only a couple of locations in Europe (most notably Marseille, FR). MAYV causes long persisting complaints, which is the most crippling feature of the disease. No specific treatment is known. In developed countries, patients run into problems regarding disability insurance (AOW) as it is not a recognized disease (much like Post-Lyme disorder). As we already knew, there is most likely an underrecognition of MAYV, due to the similarities to Dengue and Chickungunya. For the patient this doesn’t matter that much, as no specific treatment is present, but on a global health scale this is significant. For specific identification of the virus, antibodies are not convincing enough (crossreactivitiy). The “golden standard” is Serum Dilution Test Several small outbreaks of Alphaviruses have been found in Europe, mostly Chickungunya (France, Italy). This has to do with increased globalization and climate change (as long as the vector and virus can live together in the right climate, an epidemic can occur).Chickungunya can be lethal (encephalitis) to neonates and elderly. Chickungunya has already been found in NL. Mayaro can become dangerous (although it is rarely lethal) when it changes vector (seems unlikely at the moment, although similar viruses have done it) -- > it can change to Aedes which is urban dwelling and thus reach way more people. The lack of immunity in Europe to diseases like Mayaro and Chickungunya can improve likeliness of an outbreak.
